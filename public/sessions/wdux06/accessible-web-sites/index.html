Accessible Web Sites

Kieren Pitts

Why Accessibility Is Important

  • 10% to 20% of the world's population suffer from disability
  • Is increasing as average age of population increases
  • Many of these disabilities affect access to the Web (e.g. visual, aural, dexterity and memory problems)
  • For example, blind users can't see graphics and users with neurological difficulties might be confused by complex presentation
  • Everyone has a right to access the Web

It's The Law

  • In the UK there is a legal obligation under the Equality Act 2010 (previously covered under the Disability Discrimination Act 1995)
  • Most countries have disability discrimination laws
  • Numbers make commercial sense
  • >2 million visually impaired people in the UK; 161 million globally
  • Developing a Web Accessibility Business Case for Your Organization
  • Useful to convince clients

Why Accessibility Is Important To All Users

Accessible Web sites can be used in a variety of environments:

  • Low bandwidth, where images are slow to download (because accessible sites are usable without images)
  • Noisy, where difficult to hear audio
  • Bright, where difficult to see screen
  • Mobile phone, where display is small

Also good for search engines...

Structure of WCAG 1

From Checklist of Checkpoints for Web Content Accessibility Guidelines:

  • Priority 1 - "A Web content developer must satisfy this checkpoint. Otherwise, one or more groups will find it impossible to access information in the document. Satisfying this checkpoint is a basic requirement for some groups to be able to use Web documents."

    Also called "A" or "single A" checkpoints.

Structure of WCAG 1

  • Priority 2 - "A Web content developer should satisfy this checkpoint. Otherwise, one or more groups will find it difficult to access information in the document. Satisfying this checkpoint will remove significant barriers to accessing Web documents."

    Also called "AA" or "double A" checkpoints.
  • Priority 3 - "A Web content developer may address this checkpoint. Otherwise, one or more groups will find it somewhat difficult to access information in the document. Satisfying this checkpoint will improve access to Web documents."

    Also called "AAA" or "triple A" checkpoints.

Structure of WCAG 2

  • Decreased focus on technology and increased focus on principles
  • Aimed at many different groups of people and not just developers
  • Four layers of guidance:
    • Principles
    • Guidelines
    • Success criteria
    • Sufficient and Advisory Techniques - examples of techniques used to meet each success criteria

WCAG 2 Principles

Four principles provide a foundation for Web accessibility. Content must be:

  • Perceivable - information must be perceivable; not hidden to all of a user's senses
  • Operable - user interface components and navigation must be operable
  • Understandable - users must be able to understand the information presented and the user interface
  • Robust - content must be reliably interpreted by a variety of user agents (Web browsers, mobile phones, screen readers etc...)

If any of these principles are not adhered to then a disabled user will not be able to access the content.

WCAG 2 Guidelines

There are a total of twelve guidelines under the four principles:

  • Perceivable
    • Provide text alternatives for any non-text content so that it can be changed into other forms people need, such as large print, Braille, speech, symbols or simpler language
    • Provide alternatives for time-based media
    • Create content that can be presented in different ways (for example, simpler layout) without losing information or structure
    • Make it easier for users to see and hear content, including separating foreground from background

WCAG 2 Guidelines

  • Operable
    • Make all functionality available from a keyboard
    • Provide users enough time to read and use content
    • Do not design content in a way that is known to cause seizures
    • Provide ways to help users navigate, find content, and determine where they are

WCAG 2 Guidelines

  • Understandable
    • Make text content readable and understandable
    • Make Web pages appear and operate in predictable ways
    • Help users avoid and correct mistakes
  • Robust
    • Maximize compatibility with current and future user agents, including assistive technologies

WCAG 2 Success Criteria

  • Broadly analogous with the checkpoints of WCAG 1 but WCAG 2 Success Criteria are testable to ensure conformance
  • Three levels of conformance:
    • A - most important success criteria to meet. Failure to do so will present a barrier to one or more groups of users.
    • AA
    • AAA
  • A is lowest, AAA is highest
  • For content to claim 'AAA' conformance then it will have to meet all A, AA and AAA success criteria
  • Even content that conforms at the highest level (AAA) will not be accessible to individuals with all types, degrees, or combinations of disability, particularly in the cognitive language and learning areas

What Guidelines should you use?

The W3C suggest:

Although it is possible to conform either to WCAG 1.0 or to WCAG 2.0 (or both), the W3C recommends that new and updated content use WCAG 2.0. The W3C also recommends that Web accessibility policies reference WCAG 2.0.

Managers should be aware of BS 8878.

What conformance level should you use?

  • Conformance level is often determined by the content being presented and what techniques are available to make it accessible
  • In general, meeting conformance level A is considered a basic requirement for creating an accessible Web site
  • That said, most (good) developers aim to meet all the A and AA success criteria and as many of the AAA criteria as is practicable
  • The W3C say: It is not recommended that Level AAA conformance be required as a general policy for entire sites because it is not possible to satisfy all Level AAA Success Criteria for some content

Conformance Level A

  • 1.1.1 - All non-text content that is presented to the user has a text alternative that serves the equivalent purpose
    (for example, alt attribute for images; use alt="" if image isn't important) - example
  • 2.3.1 - Web pages do not contain anything that flashes more than three times in any one second period - example
  • 1.4.1 - Color is not used as the only visual means of conveying information.

More Conformance Level A

  • 4.1.1 - In content implemented using markup languages, elements have complete start and end tags, elements are nested according to their specifications, elements do not contain duplicate attributes, and any IDs are unique ("Use valid HTML" was Priority 2 in WCAG 1)
  • 1.3.2 - When the sequence in which content is presented affects its meaning, a correct reading sequence can be programmatically determined ("Don't use tables for layout unless the tables make sense when linearised" was Priority 2 in WCAG 1)

Conformance Level AA

  • 1.3.1 - Information, structure, and relationships conveyed through presentation can be programmatically determined (e.g. use <th> to identify header cells, not <td><b>; also covers using headings appropriately)
  • 2.1.1 - All functionality of the content is operable through a keyboard interface - example
  • (WCAG 1 had a Priority 1 checkpoint that said "Ensure pages are usable when scripts are turned off". In WCAG 2 there is no requirement for pages to work when scripts are turned off but scripts must be accessible - although it's advisable to still check pages with scripts turned off for other reasons.)

More Conformance Level AA

  • 1.4.3 - The visual presentation of text and images of text has a contrast ratio of at least 4.5:1 - example (High contrast, not low contrast is also important in bright environments, not just for users with poor vision.)
  • 1.4.5 - Text is used to convey information rather than images of text
  • 1.4.4 - Except for captions and images of text, text can be resized without assistive technology up to 200 percent without loss of content or functionality

More Conformance Level AA

  • 2.2.2 - For any moving, blinking or scrolling information there is a mechanism for the user to pause, stop, or hide
  • 2.2.1 - Timing Adjustable: provide a mechanism to request an update of the content instead of updating automatically
    <meta http-equiv="Refresh" content="30">
  • Also applies to redirections (except on the server)
    <meta http-equiv="Refresh" content="30; URL=http://google.com/">

More Conformance Level AA

  • 2.4.5 - More than one way is available to locate a Web page within a set of Web pages (for example, include a site map or table of contents - useful for all users, but especially those with cognitive difficulties)
  • 3.2.3 - Navigational mechanisms that are repeated on multiple Web pages occur in the same relative order each time they are repeated - example
  • 3.2.4 - Components that have the same functionality within a set of Web pages are identified consistently

Conformance Level AAA

  • 3.1.4 - A mechanism for identifying the expanded form or meaning of abbreviations is available
  • 1.4.6 - Contrast (Enhanced) - The visual presentation of text and images of text has a contrast ratio of at least 7:1
  • 1.2.6 - Sign language interpretation is provided for all prerecorded audio content in synchronized media
  • 2.4.8 - Location: Information about the user's location within a set of Web pages is available
