Assignment

The Web Development and User Experience assignment must be submitted by Tuesday, Jan 9 2018. Also, there are a number of milestones that must be completed by specific dates.

Assignment Details

This assignment tests your analytical, technical and user experience skills. The topic for your assignment must be agreed with your tutors.

The assignment should be between 4,000 and 6,000 words. You may use tables, diagrams, screenshots, code and images to explain your work clearly and concisely.

In this assignment you will produce a detailed functional specification document for your project. It will describe:

  • user and business requirements
  • the proposed information architecture
  • your chosen evaluation methods
  • project plan and costing

You will then use the information in your functional specification to mock up and develop two sample pages for your Web site.

Your assignment must demonstrate that you have a clear understanding of:

  • user-centred design
  • information architecture
  • Web usability
  • Web standards
  • HTML and CSS
  • accessibility

Your assignment should be fully reviewed by you to ensure accuracy, consistency and completeness. It should be in a form that presents a professional approach - well presented, succinct and lacking in ambiguity. You should show evidence of original user research, evaluation and iterations in design that address the findings of the research and evaluation. You should critically discuss and correctly reference your source materials.

Deliverables

Your assignment submission must contain:

  1. Your functional specification document, which will cover the following topics:
    • User research and business objectives: Explain the business objectives for your chosen project. Describe your target users and their needs. Critically discuss your approach to user research in contrast to other methods. Explain how you aim to bring user needs and business objectives together as an overall value proposition with specific site goals. Critically review some similar sites and describe what you can learn from them.
    • Design: Document the proposed information architecture of your site by using appropriate techniques, showing the overall structure (site map), specific task flows, as well as the individual pages (wireframes). Critically discuss how you solved the design problem on a conceptual as well as a detailed level. Justify your solution both in terms of user needs and usability principles. Define content requirements.
    • Testing: Describe how you tested your design and critically discuss your chosen approach and its relative strengths and weaknesses in contrast to other methods. Describe, discuss and prioritise your findings and show how they will be reflected in the final design. You may choose to test your design with users, or use an inspection technique. Either way, you need to explain and justify your approach considering the theoretical background as well as the practical application.
    • Project planning: Include a high level project plan that describes the different project activities and timeframes, resources and costs.
  2. Mock-ups of your home page and one other page of your site. The mock-ups can be drawn by hand or digitally. They should include:
    • Global navigation
    • Local navigation (if appropriate)
    • Title and headings
    • Footer information
    • Any other appropriate navigation tools
  3. HTML code, produced using Brackets or another similar editor, for your home page and one other page. Including HTML comments.
  4. CSS code, produced using Brackets or another similar editor. Including CSS comments.
  5. Screenshots of both pages taken in two different Web browsers (i.e. four screenshots in total).
  6. Supporting documentation describing the design and development of your sample pages. The documentation should demonstrate your understanding of Web standards and your wider reading.

Milestones

Milestone 1

By Tuesday, Oct 17 2017 (WDUX02) you must have selected a topic for your assignment project. The requirements are:

  • The project should be specific enough to cater for a well-defined audience with specific needs and interests. Avoid a project that is too large in scope to be successfully realised or has an audience too vague to design for.
  • You should have easy access to project stakeholders for interviews designed to elicit business objectives and functional requirements for the Web site. If the project is for you (for example, a Web site for your sole trader business) then you should be able to articulate the objectives and success criteria.
  • You should have ready access to at least 5 or 6 research participants that represent the target audience for the Web site. These participants must be available for interviews, which, at a minimum, can be conducted by phone, but face-to-face is best. These interviews are to develop an understanding of the needs and attitudes of your target audience.
  • The project should have a level of technical complexity that can be achieved during the course.

Please discuss your chosen project with the tutors during WDUX02.

Milestone 2

By Tuesday, Oct 31 2017 (WDUX04) you need to have answered the following questions:

  1. Who is the client and what are the client's objectives for this project?

    Make a list of things that the client wants to achieve through the project.

    Refer back to the slides and the reading if you are unsure how to do this.

  2. Are there any other stakeholders in the process? If yes, who?

    Conduct a stakeholder analysis. Build a communication plan.

  3. What is the value proposition of your Web site?

    That's like a mission statement for your project. Sum it up in one sentence. It should contain the following components:

    • What: What is the site?
    • Who: Who provides it and who is the audience?
    • How: How does it deliver value to the user and the business?

    For example: anaesthesia.info is a Web site for the NHS (client) that informs concerned patients (audience) about different types of anaesthesia (topic) by providing a wiki with frequently asked questions (features).

    Don't just stick to this example format; come up with a format that fits your project. Don't aim for perfection first time - you may discover that your value proposition changes as you do your research.

  4. Who is the target audience for your site?

    Look at the target audience and how the needs of different visitor types differ. Define different types of users and their anticipated needs, and where you think you need to do more research.

  5. What are the objectives of your user research? What do you want to find out about your target audience?

    Define research objectives as bullets; the "known unknowns". Be aware that these may change as you start your research and you might discover some "unknown unknowns".

  6. Research the market for your site? Are there similar sites, competitors or sites you can draw ideas from?

    Review for common features and content, and any good ideas.

A few tips:

  • Keep a journal of your research, ideas and decisions to aid your memory and document your research and design progress.
  • Write things up as you go along to make your final assignment easier. Write down findings, people you talked to, processes, issues you had conducting your research. Remember that the reader of your assignment may know nothing about your project.
  • Don't start making shiny objects at this stage: substance is more important than presentation.
  • Don't be afraid of asking more questions!
  • Don't be afraid of failure! Finding out that some of your project assumptions are wrong is an essential part of the design process. This part of the project is about de-risking your project by re-aligning your ideas with business requirements and user needs.

Milestone 3

By Tuesday, Nov 7 2017 (WDUX05) you need to have answered the following questions (as well as the above ones):

  1. Who are the users of your site?

    Look at the target audience and how the needs of different visitor types differ. Define different types of users and their anticipated needs, and where you think you need to do more research. Start working on a basic segmentation by demographics and needs.

  2. What are the objectives of your user research? What do you want to find out about your target audience?

    Define research objectives as bullets; the "known unknowns". Be aware that these may change as you start your research and you might discover some "unknown unknowns".

  3. How are you going to conduct your research? Make a research plan. Where/what method/who with/what equipment?

  4. Build a discussion guide. What questions do you know you want to ask?

Further milestones will be added when appropriate.
